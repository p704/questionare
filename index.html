Title: Identification of Common Stress Factors and Psychoactive Drug Use Among English Program Dental Students at Semmelweis University

Dear Dental Students,

We are excited to extend an invitation to participate in a ground-breaking research study aimed at enhancing our understanding of the mental well-being of dental university students. By participating in this study, you will:

  • Contribute to cutting-edge research aimed at improving the mental well-being of dental university students.
  • Help identify common stress factors and patterns of psychoactive substance use within the dental student community.
  • Potentially influence the development of targeted support services and interventions tailored to the needs of dental students.

1. Participation: Participation in this study is entirely voluntary. Your decision to participate or not will not affect your academic standing or relationship with the Semmelweis Dental University. Prior to participation, participants will receive written information about the research, and a consent form must be accepted by clicking the checkbox at the bottom of the consent form. Enclosed within the recruitment email, you, as a participant, can commence the questionnaire by simply clicking on the provided link. Following a thorough review of the current information sheet, by agreeing to the consent statement, you unlock the opportunity to complete the questionnaires online, thereby safeguarding your anonymity. The questionnaires are presented sequentially until you've provided all necessary information. Your participation in the survey does not require the disclosure of your name/personal information/email address, and no consent signature is requested as online completion prohibits it. Each participant is assigned a unique code (ID number) randomly, ensuring non-traceability. We ensure your complete anonymity and untraceability throughout the research process.

2. Withdraw: You have the right to withdraw from the study at any time without penalty.

3. The aim of the study: this study seeks to identify common stress factors and patterns of psychoactive substance use among dental students.

4. The primary objectives of this study are to:

  • Assess stress and depression levels among dental university students.
  • Detect patterns of psychoactive substance use within the dental student community.
  • Identify common stress factors experienced by dental students.
  • Explore early maladaptive schemas contributing to stress and mental health challenges.

5. Research duration: the data collection and the statistical analysis will last approximately 1 year

6. Study conduction: The questionnaires can be filled out online. The invitation to the examination is carried out using the recruiting e-mail via the Neptun platform. Conducted in collaboration with the Departments of Oro-Maxillofacial Surgery and Stomatology, as well as Department of Psychiatry and Psychotherapy at Semmelweis University, this study seeks to identify common stress factors and patterns of psychoactive substance use among dental students.

7. Participants: English-language program Dentistry students will be selected for the research. First-, second-, third-, fourth-, and fifth-year students who are currently pursuing an active semester in the year of the clinical examination can take part in the study. Number of participants: 300 students

8. Potential Risks and Benefits: There are no risks associated with participating in this study. The potential benefits include contributing valuable information that may help improve the mental well-being of dental university students.

9. Finances: Please note that this study does not have any sponsorship, and participants are not required to pay any fees. Your participation is invaluable to us, and we greatly appreciate your involvement in advancing scientific knowledge. Thank you for considering joining us in this important endeavour.

10. Research data management: Your privacy and confidentiality are of utmost importance to us. All data collected during the study will be treated with the utmost confidentiality. Each participant will be assigned a unique identification (ID) that will not be linked to your personal identity during the statistical analysis of the study. Personal information will remain anonymous, and only statistical data will be used for publication to ensure participant privacy.

DASS-21

Depression:

Depression is a mental health disorder characterized by persistent feelings of sadness, hopelessness, and a lack of interest or pleasure in activities once enjoyed. It often affects a person's thoughts, emotions, behaviors, and overall well-being. Physical symptoms such as changes in sleep, appetite, and energy levels are also common. Depression can vary in severity and duration, and it may require treatment such as therapy, medication, or lifestyle changes to manage.

Anxiety:

Anxiety is a mental health condition characterized by excessive worry, fear, or nervousness about future events or situations. It often involves feelings of unease, tension, and physical symptoms like a racing heart, sweating, trembling, or difficulty breathing. While some anxiety is a normal response to stress, excessive or chronic anxiety can interfere with daily life and may require treatment through therapy, medication, or lifestyle changes.

Stress:

Stress is the body's natural response to a perceived challenge or threat. It triggers a "fight-or-flight" reaction, leading to physical symptoms like increased heart rate, muscle tension, and heightened alertness. While short-term stress can be motivating or protective, chronic or excessive stress can negatively impact physical and mental health, contributing to issues like headaches, fatigue, anxiety, and weakened immune function. Managing stress often involves relaxation techniques, exercise, and time management strategies.

YSQ-S3

The following schemas were identified based on your answers:

No schema

In schema therapy, when no schema was identified, it means that the assessment did not reveal any significant, persistent patterns of maladaptive beliefs or behaviors typically associated with schemas. This could indicate that the individual does not have deeply ingrained negative schemas that are affecting their life in a pervasive way, or that their issues might not be schema-related. In such cases, therapy might focus on other aspects of psychological well-being, such as situational stressors, specific life challenges, or developing coping strategies, rather than addressing underlying schemas.

Emotional deprivation (ed)

The Emotional Deprivation schema in schema therapy involves a belief that one’s emotional needs will never be adequately met by others. Individuals with this schema often feel unloved, unappreciated, or emotionally neglected, and they may believe that others are unwilling or unable to provide the care, understanding, or attention they need. This can lead to feelings of emptiness, loneliness, and a reluctance to seek emotional support. The schema typically develops from early experiences where emotional needs were ignored or inadequately met by caregivers, leading to a persistent expectation of emotional deprivation in relationships.

Abandonment (ab)

The Abandonment schema in schema therapy involves an intense fear that significant others will leave, abandon, or become emotionally unavailable, leading to feelings of instability and insecurity in relationships. Individuals with this schema may worry excessively about being abandoned, become overly clingy, or feel devastated at the prospect of losing someone important to them. This can lead to difficulties in maintaining healthy relationships and may cause chronic anxiety about being alone. The schema typically develops from early experiences of loss, separation, or inconsistent emotional support, creating a deep-seated belief that relationships are fragile and people will eventually leave.

Mistrust (ma)

The Mistrust schema in schema therapy involves a pervasive expectation that others will intentionally hurt, deceive, or take advantage of you. Individuals with this schema often assume that people have hidden motives and may be suspicious, guarded, or defensive in relationships. This can lead to difficulties in trusting others, forming close connections, and feeling safe in interpersonal interactions. The schema usually develops from early experiences of betrayal, abuse, or violation of trust, leading to a belief that the world is unsafe and that others cannot be trusted.

Social isolation (si)

The Social Isolation schema in schema therapy refers to a belief that one is fundamentally different from others and does not belong to any group or community. Individuals with this schema often feel lonely, detached, or excluded, and may perceive themselves as outsiders who are disconnected from the social world. This can lead to avoiding social situations, difficulty forming close relationships, and a persistent sense of alienation. The schema often develops from early experiences of feeling isolated, rejected, or excluded by peers or family, reinforcing the belief that one is different and does not fit in.

Defectiveness (ds)

The Defectiveness schema in schema therapy involves a pervasive feeling of being flawed, unworthy, or inherently defective in some fundamental way. Individuals with this schema often believe that if others truly knew them, they would be rejected or unloved. This can lead to feelings of shame, self-loathing, and a tendency to hide or overcompensate for perceived flaws. The schema typically develops from early experiences of criticism, rejection, or emotional neglect, leading to a deep sense of inadequacy and fear of being exposed as unlovable or unworthy.

Failure (fa)

The Failure schema in schema therapy is characterized by a deep-seated belief that one is fundamentally inadequate, incapable, or destined to fail in achieving success, whether in work, school, or personal goals. Individuals with this schema often feel inferior to others, expect failure, and may avoid challenges or opportunities due to a fear of confirming their perceived incompetence. This schema typically develops from early experiences of repeated criticism, unrealistic expectations, or comparisons to others, leading to a persistent sense of being a failure or not measuring up.

Dependence/Incompetence (di)

The Dependence/Incompetence schema in schema therapy involves a belief that one is incapable of handling everyday responsibilities or making decisions without significant help from others. Individuals with this schema often feel helpless, inadequate, or overly reliant on others for guidance and support, doubting their ability to function independently. This can lead to excessive dependency in relationships and a lack of confidence in one's abilities. The schema usually stems from early experiences where the individual was overly protected, discouraged from acting independently, or made to feel incapable of managing tasks on their own.

Vulnerability to harm (vh)

The Vulnerability to Harm schema in schema therapy is characterized by an overwhelming fear that catastrophe is imminent and that one is at constant risk of experiencing disaster, illness, financial collapse, or other forms of harm. Individuals with this schema often worry excessively about potential dangers and may take extreme precautions to avoid perceived threats. This can lead to chronic anxiety and hypervigilance. The schema typically develops from early experiences where the individual was exposed to unpredictable or traumatic events, or where they were overly protected, leading to a belief that the world is a dangerous place and that harm is inevitable.

Enmeshment (em)

The Enmeshment schema in schema therapy refers to an excessive emotional involvement and closeness with one or more significant others, often to the point where one's own sense of identity and autonomy is compromised. Individuals with this schema may feel an intense need for closeness, often at the expense of developing their own independent sense of self. This can lead to difficulties in making decisions, setting boundaries, or knowing who they are outside of the relationship. The schema usually develops from early experiences where personal boundaries were blurred, and the individual was overly reliant on or intertwined with a caregiver or family member, leading to a sense of being unable to function independently.

Subjugation (sb)

The Subjugation schema in schema therapy involves a pattern of surrendering one's own needs, desires, and opinions to others, often out of fear of conflict, rejection, or punishment. Individuals with this schema may feel compelled to comply with others' demands or expectations, suppressing their own voice and autonomy. This can lead to feelings of helplessness, resentment, and a sense of being trapped. The schema typically develops from early experiences where one’s needs or opinions were consistently overruled, dismissed, or met with negative consequences, leading to a belief that asserting oneself is dangerous or futile.

Self-sacrifice (ss)

The Self-Sacrifice schema in schema therapy is characterized by an excessive focus on meeting the needs of others at the expense of one's own needs and well-being. Individuals with this schema often prioritize others' happiness and comfort, sometimes to the point of neglecting or ignoring their own desires and health. This behavior is usually driven by a desire to avoid feelings of guilt or to prevent others from suffering, but it can lead to resentment, burnout, and a lack of self-care. This schema often stems from early experiences where the individual felt responsible for the well-being of others or where their own needs were devalued.

Emotional inhibition (ei)

The Emotional Inhibition schema in schema therapy involves a tendency to suppress or restrain one's emotions, impulses, or needs out of fear of negative consequences, such as disapproval, conflict, or loss of control. Individuals with this schema often avoid expressing their feelings, especially those perceived as negative or vulnerable, and may strive to appear calm, composed, or unemotional. This can lead to emotional numbness, difficulty connecting with others, and internalized stress. The schema usually develops from early experiences where emotional expression was discouraged, criticized, or punished, leading to a belief that showing emotions is dangerous or unacceptable.

Unrelenting standards (us)

The Unrelenting Standards schema in schema therapy is characterized by a pervasive belief that one must meet extremely high standards of performance or behavior, often to avoid criticism or failure. Individuals with this schema tend to be perfectionistic, overly critical of themselves, and constantly striving for achievement, often at the expense of their own well-being or happiness. This schema can lead to chronic stress, burnout, and difficulty enjoying life. It typically arises from early experiences where love or approval was contingent on achieving high standards, leading to a belief that one's worth is tied to their accomplishments.

Entitlement/Superiority (et)

The Entitlement/Superiority schema in schema therapy involves a belief that one is inherently special, deserving of more privileges, or should be treated better than others. Individuals with this schema may feel they are above rules, expect others to cater to their needs, or view themselves as more important or competent than those around them. This can lead to difficulties in relationships, as they may struggle with empathy, cooperation, or respecting boundaries. This schema often develops from early experiences of being overly indulged or excessively praised, leading to an inflated sense of self-importance.

Insufficient self-control/self-discipline (is)

The Insufficient Self-Control/Self-Discipline schema in schema therapy refers to difficulties in exercising self-control and maintaining self-discipline. Individuals with this schema struggle to delay gratification, tolerate frustration, or persist in tasks that require sustained effort or discomfort. This can lead to problems with procrastination, impulsivity, or difficulty achieving long-term goals. The schema often stems from early experiences where limits and boundaries were either too lax or inconsistent, leading to a belief that discomfort or effort should be avoided rather than endured.

Admiration/recognition seeking (as)

The Admiration/Recognition Seeking schema in schema therapy involves a strong need for attention, approval, and validation from others. Individuals with this schema often base their self-worth on external validation, striving to be admired or recognized for their achievements, appearance, or other attributes. This can lead to behaviors aimed at gaining praise or admiration, sometimes at the expense of authentic self-expression. This schema typically arises from early experiences where love or approval was conditional, leading to a belief that one must continually prove their worth to be valued by others.

Worry/Pessimism (np)

The Worry/Pessimism schema in schema therapy is characterized by a pervasive focus on potential negative outcomes, expecting the worst to happen, and chronic anxiety about the future. Individuals with this schema tend to overestimate risks and dangers, often feeling a sense of dread or unease even when there is little evidence to support their fears. This schema may develop from early experiences of instability, unpredictability, or a sense of danger in one’s environment, leading to a deep-seated belief that things are likely to go wrong.

Self-Punitiveness (pu)

Self-Punitiveness in schema therapy refers to a maladaptive coping mechanism where an individual harshly criticizes or punishes themselves for perceived mistakes, flaws, or failures. This schema is often rooted in early experiences of excessive criticism, high expectations, or punishment from caregivers. Individuals with this schema may struggle with feelings of guilt, shame, or inadequacy, and they might believe they deserve punishment rather than compassion, leading to a cycle of self-blame and negative self-treatment.
